# Why Plain Markdown Outlasts Your Note App

Most note-taking apps come and go. The features that win you over today — a slick editor, a syncing service, an AI sidebar — are exactly the things that make your notes hard to move when the app shuts down or changes its pricing. The notes you can still read in ten years are the ones that were never trapped in the first place.

## Plain text is a format, not a feature

Markdown is just text with a few conventions: `#` for headings, `-` for lists, `[[double brackets]]` or `[links](url)` for connections. Any editor can open it, any script can parse it, and any future tool can import it. When your notes are a folder of `.md` files, the folder *is* the database — there is nothing to export because nothing was ever locked away.

## Files you control compose with tools you choose

Because the notes are plain files, you are free to point any tool at them. A desktop app can render and link them. A command-line agent can read, summarize, and cross-link them. A backup tool can version them. None of these tools owns your data; they just operate on the same folder, and you can swap any one of them out without losing a thing.

## The test that matters

Before you commit to a note system, ask one question: if this company disappeared tomorrow, could I still read and search everything I wrote? If the answer is yes, you have chosen well. If the answer depends on the company staying in business, your notes are renting space they may not always have.

Keep the format boring and the folder yours. Let the interesting tools live on top.
